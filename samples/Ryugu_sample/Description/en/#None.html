Asteroid (162173) Ryugu

Mission: Hayabusa2 (JAXA) | Type: Cb-type asteroid | Meteorite analog: CI carbonaceous chondrite

A primordial time capsule preserving the chemical evolution of ice, water, and organics from the early Solar System. The 5.4 g sample returned by Hayabusa2 in 2020 has revolutionized planetary science.

Shape & Surface

Spinning-top shape: A prominent equatorial ridge formed by YORP-driven spin-up pushing material toward the equator. Boulder density ~2× that of Itokawa.

Key landmarks: Urashima crater (largest impact crater), Cendrillon crater (largest outside equatorial ridge), Otohime Saxum (largest boulder near south pole, key to formation history)

Formation & Evolution

Phase 1: Parent body formation & aqueous alteration (~4.56 Ga)
A ~100 km parent body (Polana or Eulalia family) formed beyond the snowline. Internal heating to 0–50°C by short-lived radionuclides melted ice, driving aqueous alteration to produce hydrated clay minerals and carbonates.
Phase 2: Comet-like evolution & ice sublimation
After parent body disruption and re-accumulation, the ice-rock mixture migrated sunward. Ice sublimated, leaving behind the 41% porosity rubble-pile structure seen today.
Phase 3: Migration to near-Earth orbit & space weathering (~5 Ma ago)
GCR exposure dating places the migration from the main belt at ~5 Ma ago. Flash heating by micrometeorites caused dehydration and reduction of surface minerals, darkening the surface.

Sample Return Highlights

· Liquid water (carbonic water) trapped in crystals — direct evidence of an internal ocean in the parent body
· High-temperature particles (Ca-Al-rich inclusions) mixed in — evidence of inner-to-outer Solar System material transport
· Chemical composition matches CI chondrites — preserves the most primitive Solar System composition

Asteroid (25143) Itokawa

Mission: Hayabusa (JAXA) | Type: S-type asteroid | Meteorite analog: LL4–LL6 ordinary chondrite

A direct source of silicate rocky material that makes up terrestrial planets. A dynamic body recording a history of catastrophic disruption and re-accumulation. The microparticles returned by Hayabusa confirmed that S-type asteroids are the parent bodies of ordinary chondrite meteorites.

Shape & Surface

"Sea Otter" contact binary: Elongated, curved body composed of a "head" and "body" lobe. Surface terrain shows a sharp dichotomy:

Terrain typeRegionDescription
Rough terrainMost of head & bodyDense boulder clusters — remnants from parent body disruption
Smooth terrainMUSES SeaThick regolith deposit — Hayabusa landing & sample site

Key landmarks: MUSES Sea (smooth terrain, sample collection site), Yoshinodai (~50 m long, ~20 m tall — a direct fragment of the parent body)

Formation & Evolution

Phase 1: Parent body formation & thermal metamorphism (~4.6 Ga)
A >20 km parent body formed in the asteroid belt. Internal heating by radionuclide decay reached ~800°C, causing thermal metamorphism. U-Pb dating of phosphate minerals places this at ~4.64 Ga.
Contrast with Ryugu's parent body: low-temperature (0–50°C) aqueous alteration vs. Itokawa's high-temperature thermal metamorphism.
Phase 2: Catastrophic collision & rubble-pile formation (~1.4–1.5 Ga)
A large impactor completely shattered the 20 km parent body. Fragments re-accumulated under their own gravity into the current rubble-pile structure.
Phase 3: Contact binary formation (~<0.4 Ma ago)
The head and body lobes likely orbited each other as a binary before gently merging into the current "sea otter" shape. The narrow "neck" is the contact surface.
Phase 4: Migration to near-Earth orbit (ongoing)
Gravitational resonances with Jupiter and Mars shifted the orbit from the main belt to Earth-crossing (Apollo group). Predicted to be completely eroded or impact a planet in ~1 Ga.

Sample Return Highlights

· S-type = LL ordinary chondrite parent body confirmed by direct mineralogical and oxygen-isotope analysis
· Space weathering mechanism revealed: nanoscale metallic iron particles at the outermost surface (few nm depth), formed by solar wind implantation, cause the spectral reddening
· Rapid surface turnover: solar wind noble gas analysis shows surface exposure of only hundreds to thousands of years — regolith constantly escapes the weak gravity field. Surface eroding at ~tens of cm per Myr
